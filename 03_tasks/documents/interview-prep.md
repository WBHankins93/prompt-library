# Interview Preparation

## What This Task Does

Helps you walk into an interview with a clear narrative, prepared answers to likely questions, and an honest understanding of where you're strong and where you'll need to handle it live — so you're ready, not rehearsed.

## When to Use This

- You have an interview coming up and want to prepare systematically
- You've been interviewing without success and want to identify what's going wrong
- You're interviewing for a role that stretches your background (new industry, higher level)
- You want to prepare for tough or uncomfortable questions, not just the easy ones
- You want to know what questions to ask the interviewer

## Instructions

Copy the prompt below and paste it into your AI assistant. Then provide the information requested.

### The Prompt

```
I have an interview coming up and I want to prepare seriously — not just rehearse stock answers, but actually think through the hard questions and have a clear narrative for why I'm the right person for this role.

**The role:**
[Paste the full job description, or describe the role, company, and what they're looking for]

**My background:**
[Summary of relevant experience — what you've done, what you're good at, what's most applicable to this role]

**Why I want this role:**
[Be honest — what's drawing you to this specific job or company?]

**My biggest strengths for this role:**
[2-3 things you'd genuinely excel at in this position]

**My biggest vulnerabilities:**
[Where might they push back on my candidacy? What am I weaker on? What questions am I dreading?]

**Interview context:**
- [What stage is this? First screen, hiring manager, panel, final round?]
- [Who will you be talking to? HR, hiring manager, team members, senior leadership?]
- [What format? Technical assessment, behavioral, case-based, or conversational?]
- [Is there anything unusual about this company, role, or situation I should know?]

Please help me prepare by:
1. Identifying the 5-7 questions most likely to come up for this specific role
2. Preparing honest, specific answers to each — not scripts, but clear thinking
3. Flagging the 2-3 questions I'm most likely to fumble and helping me handle them
4. Preparing 3-5 smart questions to ask the interviewer
5. Giving me an honest assessment of my candidacy and what to watch out for
```

### What to Provide

Fill in the bracketed sections `[like this]` with your actual situation. The more honest you are about vulnerabilities and what you're dreading — the better the preparation. Good prep addresses the hard questions; it doesn't avoid them.

### What You'll Get

- The most likely questions for this specific role (not a generic list)
- Prepared thinking for each — your actual answers, not sample answers
- Honest handling of your weakest points — how to address them without hiding them
- Smart questions to ask the interviewer
- A candid read on your candidacy and what to watch

## Example

**Input:**
```
The role: Engineering Manager at a Series B startup. Looking for someone to lead a 6-person backend team, manage roadmap, and help build out hiring. Strong technical background required but people skills are the main gap they mentioned.

My background: 8 years as a senior backend engineer. Recently led a team of 3 informally (no title) during a transition period. No formal management experience.

Why I want it: Ready to stop being an IC. I've been informally mentoring for 2 years and want to do it as the job.

Strengths: Technical depth, strong relationships with engineers, know what good code review and architecture look like.

Vulnerabilities: No formal management title. Never done formal performance reviews. Haven't hired anyone.

Interview context: Second round, meeting the VP of Engineering and two senior engineers. Behavioral + technical architecture questions. Hybrid of technical and cultural fit.
```

**Output:**
The AI will prep likely questions (including the "no formal experience" question), honest answer frameworks, how to handle the experience gap directly, and smart questions to ask the VP of Eng.

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Paste the actual job description** — the more specific the role, the more targeted the question list
2. **Be honest about what you're dreading** — those are exactly the questions you need to prepare for
3. **Tell them the interview format** — behavioral prep differs from case-based or technical
4. **Know who you're talking to** — HR screens different than a hiring manager; senior leadership looks for different things
5. **Aim for clarity, not scripts** — the goal is to know what you actually think, so you can say it naturally

## Common Scenarios

### No formal experience in key area
Add: "I don't have [direct experience/title/credential]. Help me prepare to address this honestly without underselling what I do have."

### Explaining a gap or departure
Add: "I left [company] under [circumstance]. Help me address this honestly without oversharing or being defensive."

### Panel or multi-person interview
Add: "There will be [N] people in the interview. Help me think about how different stakeholders might see my candidacy differently."

### Technical or case-based assessment
Add: "Part of this interview involves [technical assessment / case study / whiteboard]. Help me prepare for that format specifically."

### Leadership or executive interview
Add: "This is a final-round interview with [CEO / C-suite]. Help me prepare for the level of strategic questioning I'll face."

## When to Suggest a Persona

This task works well on its own. Consider adding a persona when:

- **Business Leader** — You're interviewing for a leadership or management role; Business Leader understands how hiring decisions are made and what real leadership looks like to an operator.
- **Platform Mentor** — You're interviewing for a senior technical role; Platform Mentor can surface what a technical hiring bar actually looks like and prep you for architecture or system design questions.
- **Red Team** — You want adversarial preparation; Red Team will hit you with the toughest version of your vulnerabilities so you're not caught off guard when it counts.

## Advanced Usage

After getting your initial prep, you can ask:

- "Let's do a practice run — you ask me the question and I'll answer, then give me feedback"
- "Push harder on [specific question] — I'm still not satisfied with how I'd handle that"
- "What follow-up questions would a skeptical interviewer ask after my answer to [X]?"
- "Help me reframe [weakness/gap] more positively without being dishonest"
- "What should I research before this interview? What do I need to know about this company?"

## Important Notes

- Preparation is about clarity, not memorization — know what you think, not a script of what to say
- The toughest question usually comes from your actual vulnerability — that's where to spend most of your prep time
- Asking nothing at the end of an interview is a signal; prepare 3-5 genuine questions
- Interviewers can tell the difference between rehearsed and prepared — aim for the latter
- A strong candidate is honest about what they don't know; overclaiming is worse than a gap
