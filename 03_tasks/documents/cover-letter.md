# Cover Letter

## What This Task Does

Helps you write a cover letter that sounds like you — grounded in your actual voice, targeted to the specific role, and honest about why you want the job and what you bring to it.

## When to Use This

- You need a cover letter for a role you genuinely want
- Your current cover letter sounds generic or copy-pasted
- You're not sure how to explain your background clearly and compellingly
- You know what you want to say but don't know how to structure it
- You're changing industries or roles and need to bridge the gap in your narrative

## Instructions

Copy the prompt below and paste it into your AI assistant. Then fill in the information requested.

### The Prompt

```
I need help writing a cover letter. I want it to sound like me — not a template — and I want it to actually speak to why I'm a strong fit for this specific role.

**The role I'm applying for:**
[Paste the full job description here, or describe the role, company, and what they're looking for]

**My background:**
[Brief summary: where you've been, what you've done, what you're good at — 3-5 sentences]

**Why I want this role specifically:**
[Be honest — what draws you to this particular job, team, or company? Even "I want to do more X" is a real answer]

**Why I think I'm a fit:**
[2-3 reasons you'd be good at this — not just qualifications, but what you'd actually bring]

**What I'm worried about:**
[Gaps, weaknesses in my background, or things I'm unsure how to address — I want to tackle these, not avoid them]

**My voice:**
[How do you communicate? Direct and concise? Warm and conversational? Formal? Give an example sentence or two if helpful]

Please write a cover letter that:
1. Opens with something specific — not "I'm excited to apply"
2. Makes a clear case for why I'm right for this role
3. Addresses any obvious gaps or pivots honestly
4. Closes with a clear, direct ask
5. Sounds like a person wrote it, not a template
```

### What to Provide

Fill in the bracketed sections `[like this]` with your specific information. The more honest you are — especially about gaps and concerns — the better the output. Cover letters that avoid weaknesses are less credible than ones that address them directly.

### What You'll Get

- A complete, ready-to-edit cover letter draft
- In a voice and structure appropriate for the role and company
- With a clear narrative thread from opening to close
- That addresses your background honestly, including any pivots or gaps

## Example

**Input:**
```
The role: Senior Product Manager at a fintech startup building tools for freelancers. They want 5+ years PM experience, startup background preferred, and someone who's worked in financial products.

My background: 7 years in product — 4 at a mid-size SaaS company in HR tech, 3 as a PM lead at a startup (acquired). I've managed 3 PMs and launched 4 major product lines.

Why this role: I've been wanting to move into fintech for a year. The freelancer problem is personal — I freelanced for 2 years before going full-time PM. I understand that user.

Why I'm a fit: I know how to ship in resource-constrained environments. I've owned roadmap, strategy, and execution simultaneously. I have lived experience as the user they're building for.

What I'm worried about: I don't have direct fintech or financial products experience. I want to acknowledge this without making it the focus.

My voice: Direct, specific, no fluff. I like using short sentences when I'm making a point.
```

**Output:**
The AI will write a complete cover letter in your voice — specific opening, clear case for fit, honest handling of the fintech gap, and a direct close.

## Tips for Best Results

1. **Paste the full job description** — generic cover letters come from generic inputs; the more specific you are, the more targeted the output
2. **Be honest about your concerns** — a good cover letter doesn't avoid weaknesses, it contextualizes them
3. **Describe your voice** — "professional" isn't useful; "direct and warm, short sentences" is
4. **Give a real reason you want the role** — the AI can tell when you're hedging; real motivation makes for a stronger letter
5. **Plan to edit** — the draft gives you a strong foundation; your job is to make it feel even more you

## Common Scenarios

### Career change or industry pivot
Add: "I'm making a transition from [old field] to [new field]. Help me explain this as a strength, not a detour."

### Employment gap
Add: "I have a [X-month] gap in my employment history from [reason]. Help me address this directly without over-explaining."

### Overqualified
Add: "I'm applying for a role that's a step back from my current level. Help me explain why this makes sense without sounding defensive."

### No direct experience in the specific domain
Add: "I don't have experience in [domain], but I have [adjacent experience]. Help me bridge that gap honestly."

### Internal application
Add: "I'm applying internally. Help me write something that acknowledges my existing relationships while making a genuine case for the role."

## When to Suggest a Persona

This task works well on its own. However:

- **Inner Voice** — Use when you're struggling to find the right framing or your voice feels off. Inner Voice specializes in helping you express your actual self authentically.
- **Storyteller** — Use when you have a compelling narrative arc (pivot, comeback, unconventional path) that needs to land. Storyteller turns a sequence of facts into something that means something.

## Advanced Usage

After getting your initial draft, you can ask:

- "Rewrite the opening — I want something more specific to [the company / the problem they're solving]"
- "Tighten this — cut anything that doesn't serve the case I'm making"
- "Soften the tone — this reads a little aggressive for a [conservative / formal] company"
- "Add more warmth — I want it to feel like a person wrote it, not a strategist"
- "Help me adapt this letter for [different role] — same company, different position"

## Important Notes

- A one-page letter is standard; one strong page beats two weak ones
- Opening lines that start with "I" are a quick way to make it feel generic — lead with the role, the company, or the problem
- The cover letter and resume should tell the same story — inconsistency signals carelessness
- Most cover letters are skimmed in 20-30 seconds; make the first paragraph carry the weight
- If you don't know why you want the job, the letter will show it — figure that out first
