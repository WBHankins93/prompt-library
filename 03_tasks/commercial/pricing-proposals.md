# Pricing and Proposals

> How to price web development work so you stay profitable and clients feel they got value.
> Covers fixed-price, retainer, and value-based models.

---

## Pricing Philosophy

Undercharging is the default failure mode for freelancers and small agencies. You lose money on projects, resent the work, deliver less because you can't afford to spend time on quality, and the client gets a worse product. Pricing well is a service to both parties.

Your price should reflect the value delivered, not the hours spent. A simple 5-page site for a plumber that generates $5K/month in new business is worth more than a complex app that sits unused.

---

## Pricing Models

### Fixed Price (Recommended Default)

You quote a flat price for a defined scope. Client knows the cost upfront. You're incentivized to work efficiently.

**When to use:** Client sites with clear requirements. Marketing sites, business websites, landing pages, small e-commerce stores.

**When to avoid:** Vague scope, uncertain requirements, "we'll figure it out as we go" projects. These need hourly or retainer pricing.

**How to price:** Estimate your hours, multiply by your target hourly rate, then add a buffer.

```
Estimated hours: 40
Target hourly rate: $125
Base price: $5,000
Complexity buffer (20%): $1,000
Fixed price: $6,000
```

The buffer accounts for scope ambiguity, client communication overhead, and revisions. Projects rarely take fewer hours than estimated.

### Hourly Rate

You bill for time spent. Simple to explain, simple to track.

**When to use:** Ongoing work, consulting, debugging, maintenance tasks, projects with undefined scope.

**When to avoid:** Full site builds where the client wants a number upfront. Hourly billing creates anxiety — the client worries about every email they send you.

**Rate setting:** Your hourly rate should account for non-billable time (sales, admin, learning, marketing). If you want to earn $150K/year and you bill 1,200 hours/year (60% utilization), your rate is $125/hour.

### Retainer

Client pays a fixed monthly fee for a set number of hours or defined services.

**When to use:** Ongoing maintenance, content updates, continuous development. See maintenance tiers in [`../tech-engineer/monitoring-upkeep.md`](../tech-engineer/monitoring-upkeep.md).

**Structure:** Define hours or deliverables, not just "access to you." Unused hours don't roll over (or cap rollover at one month). Overage billed at your standard hourly rate.

### Value-Based Pricing

Price based on the economic value to the client, not your cost to deliver.

**When to use:** When you can quantify the business impact. A website for a law firm that brings in $50K/year in new cases can be priced at $10-15K, regardless of whether it takes you 30 hours or 60.

**How to frame it:** "This site will generate an estimated $X in new revenue per year. My fee is $Y — you'll earn that back in Z months."

**Requires:** Confidence in the value you deliver, a client who understands ROI, and enough projects to absorb the occasional one that doesn't perform as expected.

## Pricing Tiers (2026 Market Rates)

These are baseline ranges for the US market. Adjust for your location, experience, and client type.

### Small Business Website (5-10 pages, brochure/marketing site)

**Budget tier:** $2,000-4,000
- Template-based (WordPress theme, Squarespace, or Webflow)
- Client provides all content
- Minimal customization
- 2-3 week delivery

**Standard tier:** $5,000-10,000
- Custom design (Figma mockups → code)
- CMS for client self-service (WordPress, Sanity, or similar)
- Responsive, performance-optimized
- Basic SEO setup
- 4-6 week delivery

**Premium tier:** $10,000-20,000
- Full custom design with branding guidance
- Advanced functionality (booking systems, member areas, complex forms)
- Copywriting or content strategy included
- Performance and accessibility optimization
- 6-10 week delivery

### E-Commerce

**Small store (under 50 products):** $5,000-15,000
- Shopify or WooCommerce
- Custom theme, product setup, payment integration

**Mid-size store (50-500 products):** $15,000-40,000
- Custom design, advanced filtering, inventory management
- Integration with fulfillment, accounting, CRM

### Web Application / SaaS MVP

**Simple MVP:** $15,000-40,000
- User auth, core feature set (3-5 main features), basic admin panel
- 8-16 week delivery

**Full MVP:** $40,000-100,000+
- Complex business logic, integrations, multi-role access
- 3-6 month delivery

## Proposal Structure

A proposal is a sales document. It should make the client confident that you understand their problem and can solve it.

### Section 1: Understanding (1 paragraph)

Restate their problem in their words. Demonstrate you listened during discovery.

> "You're a family-owned plumbing company with 15 years of experience and a strong reputation, but your current website doesn't reflect the quality of your work. You're losing potential customers to competitors who show up better on Google and have sites that build trust at first glance."

### Section 2: Solution (2-3 paragraphs)

What you'll build and why. Focus on outcomes, not technology.

> "I'll build a modern, fast-loading website designed to convert visitors into service calls. The site will showcase your work with a professional portfolio, make it easy to request a quote, and position you well for local search results."

### Section 3: Scope (bulleted list)

Reference the scope document. List what's included. List what's explicitly not included.

### Section 4: Investment

Don't call it "cost" — it's an investment in their business.

```
Website design and development:    $7,500

Payment schedule:
  - 50% deposit to begin work:     $3,750
  - 50% upon launch:               $3,750

Optional ongoing maintenance:      $100/month
  (see attached maintenance plan)
```

**Present one recommended option, not three.** If you offer three tiers, the client picks the cheapest one and resents not getting the features in the expensive one. If you have one recommendation, they either say yes or negotiate.

**Exception:** If the client's budget is genuinely uncertain, present two options: "Here's what I'd recommend" and "Here's a lighter version that fits a tighter budget." Never more than two.

### Section 5: Timeline

```
Week 1:     Content collection and design mockups
Week 2:     Client feedback on designs
Week 3-4:   Development
Week 5:     Review, revisions, and launch preparation
Week 6:     Launch

Note: Timeline assumes content is provided by [date].
Delays in content delivery will shift the launch date accordingly.
```

### Section 6: Next Steps

Clear call to action. What do they do to move forward?

> "To get started, sign the attached agreement and submit the deposit via [payment method]. I'll follow up within 24 hours with a content questionnaire and schedule our kickoff call."

### Section 7: Terms

Payment terms, revision policy, cancellation terms. Or reference the full contract attached separately.

## Payment Structure

### Standard Split

**50/50:** 50% deposit, 50% at launch. Simple, works for most projects.

**40/30/30:** 40% deposit, 30% at design approval, 30% at launch. Better for larger projects — reduces your risk of doing 80% of the work before the second payment.

### Non-Negotiable Rules

- **Never start work without a deposit.** If they won't pay a deposit, they won't pay the final invoice.
- **Never deliver the final site before final payment.** Present it on a staging URL. Transfer to their domain upon payment.
- **Payment terms are net-15.** Not net-30, not "when we get around to it." Late payments incur a fee (1.5%/month is standard).
- **Accept multiple payment methods.** Stripe invoicing, QuickBooks, or Wise for international. Don't lose a deal because you only take checks.

### Handling "That's Too Expensive"

This isn't a negotiation failure — it's a qualification failure. Either the client can't afford what they need, or you haven't communicated the value clearly enough.

**Options:**
- Reduce scope to fit their budget (remove features, use templates instead of custom design)
- Offer a payment plan (spread payments over 3-4 months, but front-load so you're not financing the project)
- Walk away (if the budget is genuinely below your floor, politely decline and refer them to someone who works at that price point)

**Do not:** Drop your price without reducing scope. This trains clients to negotiate and devalues your work.

## Calculating Your Floor

Your pricing floor is the minimum you can charge per project and stay solvent. Below this, you're losing money even if it feels like you're getting paid.

```
Annual expenses (business):        $15,000
Annual expenses (personal):        $60,000
Taxes (estimated 25-30%):          $25,000
Desired savings/profit:            $20,000
                                   ─────────
Annual revenue target:             $120,000

Billable hours per year:           1,200  (60% of 2,000 working hours)
Minimum hourly rate:               $100/hour

Average project hours:             50
Minimum project price:             $5,000
```

If a project prices out below your floor, either the scope is too small to be worth taking, or you need to revisit the scope.

## Proposal Delivery

- **Format:** PDF, professionally formatted. Not a Google Doc, not a plain email. The proposal is a reflection of your work quality.
- **Send within 3-5 business days** of the discovery call. Longer than that and momentum dies.
- **Follow up:** If you haven't heard back in 5 business days, one follow-up email. After 10 business days, one more. Then move on. Don't chase clients who aren't responding.
- **Track proposals:** Know your close rate. If you're closing less than 30% of proposals, either your pricing is off, your qualifying is off, or your proposals aren't compelling.
