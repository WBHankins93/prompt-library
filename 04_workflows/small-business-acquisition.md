# Small Business Acquisition Evaluation Workflow

## What This Workflow Does

Takes you from "I'm looking at a business for sale" to a clear, well-reasoned buy/pass decision — or a structured set of conditions that would need to be true to proceed.

Most acquisition mistakes happen because buyers fall in love with the idea of ownership before understanding the actual business. This workflow forces the hard questions in the right sequence: business quality first, then financial structure, then legal risk, then stress-tested conviction.

## When to Use This

- You're evaluating a specific business that's listed for sale
- You've been approached about acquiring a competitor, supplier, or complementary business
- You're searching for acquisition targets and have found a shortlist to evaluate
- You're in due diligence and want to make sure you've asked the right questions

## What You'll Need Before Starting

- **The business listing or CIM** (Confidential Information Memorandum) — whatever the seller has provided
- **Basic financials** — P&L for the last 3 years if available; revenue, expenses, owner's compensation
- **The seller's asking price and deal structure** — how much, what terms, what's included
- **Your own situation** — available capital, whether you'd operate it or hire management, your relevant background

> **New here?** See the [Getting Started guide](getting-started.md) for how to load personas and run workflow stages.

## How This Workflow Runs

**Run as a single, continuous conversation when possible.** Each stage builds on what came before. If the business fails scrutiny in Stage 1 or 2, stop — you've gotten the value without the full time investment.

**If you pause:** Use the checkpoint prompts at the end of each stage. Paste the summary at the start of your next session.

**Estimated time:** 4-6 hours total across multiple sessions. Most meaningful acquisitions deserve this.

## Stage 1: Business Quality Assessment

**Persona: Boring Gold Miner**
**Time: 45-60 minutes**

Before you model anything, before you think about price, understand what the business actually is. The Boring Gold Miner specializes in evaluating the operational and commercial quality of main-street businesses — not whether they're exciting, but whether they're durable.

```
You are my Boring Gold Miner — a persona that evaluates small businesses for operational quality, revenue durability, and hidden risk, with a deep skepticism toward sellers' presentations.

I'm evaluating a business for potential acquisition. Help me understand what I'm actually looking at.

Business overview:
[Paste the listing description, CIM summary, or your own notes on what the business is]

Financials as presented:
[Revenue, profit, owner's compensation, key expenses for last 1-3 years]

Asking price and terms:
[Price, down payment, seller financing if any, what's included]

My background and intentions:
[Will you operate it? Hire a manager? What relevant skills do you bring?]

Help me assess:
1. The revenue quality — how recurring is this? What are the customer concentration risks? What would revenue look like without the owner?
2. The operational dependencies — what breaks if the current owner leaves on day one?
3. The competitive position — why do customers choose this business over alternatives?
4. The reason for sale — what does the stated reason suggest? What are plausible unstated reasons?
5. The first-year reality — what would actually happen in year one under new ownership?
6. Initial go/no-go read — is this a business worth continuing to investigate, or are there fundamental problems that make it a pass?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Clear assessment of revenue durability and quality
- Identified operational risks and owner dependencies
- An honest read on the reason for sale
- A go/no-go recommendation on whether to continue due diligence

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the key findings from this stage: the business quality assessment, the main risks identified, and your recommendation on whether to continue. I'll use this in later stages."

## Stage 2: Financial Analysis

**Persona: Finance Dragon**
**Time: 45-60 minutes**

The Boring Gold Miner told you whether the business is worth understanding. Finance Dragon tells you whether the price makes sense and what the return actually looks like.

```
You are my Finance Dragon — a persona that cuts through financial presentations to understand what's actually happening in the numbers, and models whether a deal makes economic sense.

I'm continuing to evaluate this acquisition. We've already assessed business quality. Now I need to understand the financial reality.

Seller's financials (what they've provided):
[Revenue by year, stated owner's discretionary earnings or EBITDA, key expense lines]

My capital situation:
[Total available capital, how much I'd put down, whether I'm taking on debt or seller financing]

Questions I already have from the business review:
[Any specific financial concerns that came up in Stage 1]

Help me:
1. Reconstruct the actual financials — what do the numbers actually show versus what's being presented? What adjustments need to be made?
2. Model the acquisition economics — at the asking price, what's my actual return on invested capital at current performance? At 80% of current performance?
3. Stress-test the deal at lower performance — what happens if revenue drops 20% in year one of transition?
4. Evaluate the price — is the multiple reasonable for this type of business, this level of risk, and this level of owner dependency?
5. Structure the capital — what's the optimal structure between equity, debt, and seller financing?
6. Identify the questions I need answered to verify the financial picture — what numbers do I need to see before I can trust this model?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Reconstructed P&L with appropriate adjustments
- Return analysis at current, 80%, and 60% performance scenarios
- Clear assessment of whether the price is defensible
- A list of specific financial questions for the seller

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the financial model, the key scenarios, your assessment of the price, and the top financial questions I need to resolve. I'll use this in later stages."

## Stage 3: Legal and Risk Architecture

**Persona: Legal Advisor**
**Time: 30-45 minutes**

Finance Dragon told you if the numbers work. Legal Advisor tells you what risks you'd be inheriting and what the deal structure needs to protect you.

```
You are my Legal Advisor — a persona that identifies legal risks, contract vulnerabilities, and structural protections in business transactions.

I'm evaluating an acquisition that looks financially viable. Now I need to understand the legal landscape.

Business type and structure:
[Is it an asset purchase or stock purchase? What entity type is the seller?]

What I know about legal matters:
[Any known contracts with customers or suppliers, lease situation, employees or contractors, any disclosed legal issues]

Help me understand:
1. Asset purchase vs. stock purchase — what are the implications for liability, tax, and risk?
2. Key legal due diligence items — what documents do I need to review before closing?
3. Structural vulnerabilities — what legal risks are commonly hidden in businesses like this?
4. Representations and warranties — what should the purchase agreement require the seller to represent?
5. Holdback and indemnification — how do I structure protection against problems that emerge post-close?
6. The questions I should ask the seller's attorney

Note: This is for framework education only. I'll engage actual legal counsel before executing any transaction.
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Legal due diligence checklist for this transaction type
- Understanding of asset vs. stock purchase implications
- Key protections to include in the purchase agreement
- A clear list of questions for your actual legal counsel

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the key legal risks, the due diligence checklist, and the structural protections I should prioritize. I'll bring this to my attorney."

## Stage 4: Red Team

**Persona: Red Team**
**Time: 30-45 minutes**

You're considering committing significant capital to this business. Red Team's job is to find the assumption you're wrong about before it costs you.

```
Red Team: I need you to stress-test my acquisition thesis.

I've done the business quality assessment, the financial modeling, and the legal review. I'm leaning toward [proceeding / making an offer / declining]. I need you to challenge my reasoning.

Here's my current thinking:
[Summarize: Why you think this is a good acquisition, the price you're considering, the main risks you've identified and how you plan to manage them]

What I'm telling myself about the risks:
[The rationalizations — "the owner dependency isn't that bad because..." or "the customer concentration risk is manageable because..."]

Find:
1. The assumption I'm making that's most likely wrong
2. The scenario I'm not taking seriously enough
3. What would make this deal a disaster, and how likely is that scenario really?
4. What a seller would hide that isn't showing up in what I've reviewed
5. The question I haven't asked that I should have
6. If I'm leaning toward proceeding, what should make me reconsider? If I'm leaning toward passing, am I being too cautious?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Adversarial pressure-test of your acquisition thesis
- Identified blind spots and unconsidered scenarios
- A clear-eyed view of the downside you're accepting

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the main vulnerabilities in my thesis and the specific concerns I need to resolve before making a final decision."

## Stage 5: Final Decision and Offer Strategy

**Persona: Boring Gold Miner, Finance Dragon**
**Time: 30-45 minutes**

Synthesize everything into a clear decision and, if proceeding, a structured offer strategy.

```
I've completed the full evaluation. I need to make a final decision and, if proceeding, develop my offer strategy.

Summary of what I know:
[Paste the checkpoint summaries from previous stages, or ask the AI to recall what was discussed]

My current decision lean:
[Proceeding with an offer / Passing / Need more information on X before deciding]

If proceeding, help me:
1. Define my walkaway price — what's the maximum I'd pay and why?
2. Define my opening offer and rationale
3. Identify what I want in seller financing, earnout, or other structure
4. List the conditions I need satisfied before closing (financial verification, lease transfer, key employee retention)
5. Draft the key points of the letter of intent

If passing, help me:
1. Articulate exactly why — what specific factor made this a no?
2. Identify what would have needed to be different for this to be a yes
3. Note what I'd look for differently in the next opportunity
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- A clear, reasoned buy or pass decision
- If buying: offer price, structure, and key conditions
- If passing: clear criteria for the next opportunity

---

## Completion Criteria

This evaluation is complete when:

- [ ] Business quality is understood — revenue durability, owner dependency, competitive position
- [ ] Financial model exists — return scenarios at current, 80%, and 60% performance
- [ ] Legal risks are identified — due diligence list ready, attorney engaged or scheduled
- [ ] Red Team has challenged the thesis — no major unexamined assumptions remaining
- [ ] Decision is clear — yes with specific structure and conditions, or no with specific reasoning
- [ ] If yes: letter of intent is ready or in draft

---

## Notes

- If the business fails Stage 1 scrutiny, stop. You've done the right thing. Move to the next candidate.
- Never skip the financial stress-test at 60-80% performance. Optimistic models produce bad acquisitions.
- Red Team (Stage 4) is not optional. Every deal that has looked great on paper deserves adversarial pressure.
- This workflow does not replace actual legal counsel or a licensed accountant for due diligence. It prepares you to use that counsel effectively.
