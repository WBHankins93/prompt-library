# New Product or Business Idea Validation Workflow

## What This Workflow Does

Takes you from "I have an idea" to a clear, evidence-based read on whether it's worth building — and if so, what to build first, who to build it for, and how to validate before you've spent significant money.

Most ideas feel compelling in the head. This workflow exposes the assumptions that kill ideas before you've invested in them, and sharpens the ones worth pursuing into a testable first version.

## When to Use This

- You have a business or product idea and want to pressure-test it before committing time or money
- You've been working on something and want an honest outside read on whether the fundamentals hold
- You're choosing between multiple ideas and want a rigorous comparison
- You're early in a market and want to define the opportunity before building a solution

## What You'll Need Before Starting

- **Your idea, described honestly** — not the pitch deck version, the real version: what it is, who it's for, how it would make money
- **What you know and don't know** — be specific about which parts are assumptions vs. evidence
- **Your constraints** — time, capital, technical ability, and what success looks like to you in 12 months

> **New here?** See the [Getting Started guide](getting-started.md) for how to load personas and run each stage.

## How This Workflow Runs

**Run as a single continuous conversation.** Each stage builds on what came before. If the idea doesn't survive Stage 2's market reality check, stop — that's valuable information at low cost.

**Estimated time:** 3-4 hours total. Split naturally after Stage 3 (the audit) if needed.

---

## Stage 1: Idea Articulation and Hypothesis Building

**Persona: SaaS Founder**
**Time: 30-40 minutes**

Before anything can be validated, the idea needs to be stated precisely. Fuzzy ideas can't be tested. This stage converts a vague concept into specific, testable hypotheses.

```
You are my SaaS Founder — a persona that thinks about product-market fit, business model viability, and the assumptions that determine whether a new business works.

I have a business/product idea I want to pressure-test. Help me state it clearly before we evaluate it.

The idea:
[Describe it plainly — what it is, what problem it solves, and who it's for]

What I believe to be true about it:
[The assumptions you're building on: about the problem, the customer, the market, the business model]

What I actually know vs. what I'm assuming:
[Be honest — which parts have you verified with real customers/data, and which are your beliefs?]

What success looks like to me in 12 months:
[Revenue, users, proof of concept, something else?]

Help me:
1. State the core hypothesis — one clear sentence: "If [customer type] has [specific problem], they will pay [price] for [solution] because [value proposition]"
2. Identify the 3-5 assumptions this idea depends on, ranked by risk (most uncertain and most consequential first)
3. Name the single assumption whose failure would kill the idea — what's the riskiest bet?
4. Define what "validation" actually looks like — what evidence would tell you the idea is worth building?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- A precisely stated core hypothesis
- Ranked assumptions by risk and consequence
- A clear definition of what validation looks like

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize my core hypothesis, the ranked assumption list, and what validation looks like. I'll use this in the next stages."

## Stage 2: Market Reality Check

**Persona: Pattern Seer, Arbitrage Hunter**
**Time: 40-50 minutes**

An idea that solves a real problem can still fail because the market is too small, too saturated, or structurally unfavorable. This stage forces a clear-eyed look at the market before you build anything.

```
You are my Pattern Seer and Arbitrage Hunter — you think in market structures, competitive dynamics, and where genuine advantage exists versus where it doesn't.

I've established my core hypothesis. Now I need to understand the market reality.

My hypothesis (from Stage 1):
[State the core hypothesis and top assumptions]

Help me understand:
1. The market structure — who else is serving this customer and solving this problem? Where do they fall short?
2. The window question — is this market early (timing is the advantage), established (differentiation is the advantage), or late (most of the value has been captured)?
3. The structural advantages — what does a new entrant have going for it? What would make this approach specifically defensible over time?
4. The size reality — how many people actually have this problem, and how many are willing to pay? What's the realistic addressable market, not the optimistic one?
5. The business model fit — does the go-to-market model (how you'd acquire customers, at what cost) match the value proposition and likely price point?
6. What I'm competing against that I'm not thinking of — including "do nothing" and "good enough" existing solutions
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- A clear map of the competitive landscape
- An honest assessment of market timing and size
- Identified structural advantages (and gaps)
- First signal on business model viability

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the market reality check: competitive landscape, timing assessment, market size reality, and the most important structural factors. I'll carry this forward."

## Stage 3: Customer and Channel Clarity

**Persona: Marketing Mastermind**
**Time: 30-40 minutes**

The best product in the wrong channel never reaches the right customer. This stage defines who the actual first customer is and how to reach them — specifically enough to test.

```
You are my Marketing Mastermind — you think in customer psychology, messaging, and the specific paths that connect the right customer to the right solution.

I'm defining my customer and channel for a new product/business.

What I know about the market (from Stage 2):
[Brief summary of competitive landscape and market structure]

My hypothesis about the customer:
[Who specifically — not "small business owners" but "solo service business owners doing $100-500K in revenue who currently track projects in spreadsheets"]

Help me:
1. Narrow the customer — who is the most specific first customer? Not the eventual customer, the first one. The one who feels the pain most acutely and is most motivated to try something new.
2. Define the message — in one sentence, what do I say to that specific customer that makes them stop and pay attention?
3. Identify the channel — where does that specific customer actually go to solve this kind of problem? Where do they find tools, services, and solutions like mine?
4. Define the acquisition path — what does the customer journey from "never heard of this" to "paying customer" actually look like?
5. Identify the first 10 customers — who specifically would I talk to first? Not demographics, actual humans I could reach. How would I reach them?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- A sharply defined first customer profile
- A clear, specific value message for that customer
- An identified acquisition channel
- A concrete path to the first 10 conversations

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize my first customer definition, the message, the channel, and how I'd reach the first 10 potential customers."

## Stage 4: Validation Red Team

**Persona: Red Team**
**Time: 30-40 minutes**

You've built a coherent-sounding thesis. Red Team finds the hole before you build toward it.

```
Red Team: I need you to find what's wrong with my business idea before I commit to building it.

I've worked through the idea articulation, the market reality, and the customer/channel. I have a thesis I'm feeling good about. I need you to challenge it.

My thesis:
[Summarize the idea, the target customer, the market position, and the go-to-market approach from previous stages]

What I'm most confident about:
[The parts of the thesis you feel good about]

What I'm most uncertain about:
[The parts you know are assumptions you haven't proven]

Find:
1. The fatal flaw — the one thing, if true, that makes this not work
2. The assumption I'm least likely to question because it feels true — what if it's wrong?
3. What a well-funded competitor or established incumbent does when I enter — and whether my answer to that is actually good enough
4. The customer I'm imagining vs. the customer who actually exists — am I solving a real problem or one I believe exists?
5. The business model hole — where does the unit economics fall apart?
6. If this idea is worth pursuing, what would I need to see in the first 90 days to know it's working?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- An adversarial challenge to your strongest assumptions
- Identified fatal risks and business model gaps
- A 90-day validation metric framework

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the key vulnerabilities Red Team found and what I'd need to see in 90 days to know this is working."

---

## Stage 5: MVP Definition and First 30 Days

**Persona: SaaS Founder**
**Time: 30-40 minutes**

If the idea survived Red Team, define the minimum viable version to test it — and the concrete first-month action plan.

```
The idea has survived scrutiny. Now I need to define the smallest thing I could build or do to test the riskiest assumption — and the concrete action plan for the first 30 days.

What I'm trying to validate first (from Stage 4):
[The riskiest assumption — what needs to be true?]

My current resources:
[Time per week, capital available, technical ability]

Help me define:
1. The MVP — what's the smallest version that tests the riskiest assumption? Not the full product, the test. Could be a landing page, a manual service, a prototype, a sales conversation before anything is built.
2. The success metric — what specific outcome in the first 30 days tells me the assumption is right?
3. The first 30-day plan — specific actions, week by week, starting with customer conversations before building anything
4. What stops me before starting — the objection in my own head, and whether it's a real blocker or a fear
5. The decision rule — what happens at day 30? What does "proceed" look like vs. "pivot" vs. "stop"?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- A defined MVP that tests your riskiest assumption
- A concrete 30-day action plan
- A clear decision rule at the end of the test period

---

## Completion Criteria

Your idea is ready to act on when:

- [ ] Core hypothesis is stated precisely — one sentence, falsifiable
- [ ] Assumptions are ranked — you know which ones to test first
- [ ] Market reality is understood — size, timing, competition, business model fit
- [ ] First customer is defined — specific enough to actually find them
- [ ] Red Team has challenged the thesis — no unexamined assumptions in the high-risk tier
- [ ] MVP is defined — smallest test of the riskiest assumption
- [ ] Day-30 decision rule is clear — you know what you're looking for

---

## Notes

- If you leave Stage 2 believing your market is everyone or that you have no real competitors, Red Team in Stage 4 will find you. Better to resolve it in Stage 2.
- The goal of Stage 5's MVP is not to build the product. It's to test one assumption. Don't let the MVP become a product.
- A "no" from this workflow is a success. It's significantly less expensive than a "no" six months and $50,000 into building.
