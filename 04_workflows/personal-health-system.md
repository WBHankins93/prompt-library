# Personal Health System Build

## What This Workflow Does

Takes you from "I want to be healthier" to a specific, sustainable system — built around your actual life, your actual constraints, and the things that will actually make the most difference given where you're starting.

Most health overhauls fail because they try to change everything at once. This workflow identifies the highest-leverage starting point, builds the minimum viable system, and designs habits that will hold when motivation runs out.

## When to Use This

- You want to build or rebuild a health routine and don't know where to start
- You've tried before and it didn't stick — you want a different approach this time
- You're at a life transition (new job, move, post-injury, returning from a break) and need to rebuild your baseline
- You have a specific goal (fat loss, strength, energy, longevity) and want a coherent system to pursue it
- You want to understand what the evidence actually says about what matters before you spend money or time on it

## What You'll Need Before Starting

- **Honest current state** — what does your eating, movement, and sleep actually look like right now? Not idealized — actual.
- **Your real constraints** — time available, budget, equipment access, family situation, schedule patterns
- **The goal that matters most to you** — one primary goal is more useful than three

> **New here?** See the [Getting Started guide](getting-started.md) for how to load personas and run each stage.
> **Medical note:** If you have medical conditions, are on medications, or have symptoms that need evaluation, see your physician before making significant changes to diet or exercise. This workflow addresses wellness and optimization, not medical care.

## How This Workflow Runs

**Run as a single conversation.** Each stage builds a more specific picture of your system. Most people complete this in one 2-3 hour session and then have a written system to implement.

**Estimated time:** 2-3 hours. Stages 1-2 are assessment; Stages 3-4 are system design and habit architecture.

## Stage 1: Honest Baseline and Goal Clarity

**Persona: Health Optimizer**
**Time: 30-40 minutes**

Before any prescription, an honest diagnosis. The Health Optimizer builds systems that fit real lives — not aspirational versions of your life.

```
You are my Health Optimizer — a persona that builds sustainable, evidence-based health systems around real constraints and real lives.

I want to build a personal health system. Before you recommend anything, help me get clear on where I'm starting and what I actually need.

My current state (be honest):
- Sleep: [Hours per night, consistency, quality — do you feel rested?]
- Eating: [What does a typical day of eating actually look like? Skip nothing.]
- Movement: [What physical activity are you actually doing right now?]
- Energy: [How's your energy across the day — morning, afternoon, evening?]

My real constraints:
- Time: [How many minutes per day/week can realistically go to health behaviors?]
- Budget: [What's realistic for food and any fitness costs per month?]
- Equipment: [Full gym? Home equipment? Nothing?]
- Schedule: [When do you actually have control over your time?]
- Family/life situation: [Anything that shapes what's possible — kids, travel, job demands]

My primary goal:
[The one thing that matters most right now — fat loss, muscle gain, energy, longevity, managing a health issue, something else]

Help me:
1. Audit my current state honestly — what's actually happening here, and what's the most likely underlying cause?
2. Identify the highest-leverage single change — given where I'm starting, what one thing would have the biggest impact?
3. Clear away the noise — what should I stop worrying about until the fundamentals are solid?
4. Reality-check my goal — is it realistic given my constraints? What timeframe is realistic?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Honest current-state assessment across sleep, nutrition, and movement
- The single highest-leverage change for your specific situation
- Realistic goal framing and timeline
- What to stop worrying about for now

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize my current baseline, the highest-leverage change, and the realistic goal framing. I'll use this to build my system."

## Stage 2: Evidence Check on What Actually Matters

**Persona: Curious Scientist, Health Optimizer**
**Time: 25-35 minutes**

Most health information people act on is wellness marketing. This stage establishes what the evidence actually supports for your specific goal — and what you can safely deprioritize.

```
You are my Curious Scientist and Health Optimizer — help me understand what the evidence actually says about achieving my specific goal.

My goal (from Stage 1):
[State the primary goal]

Claims I've heard that I want reality-checked:
[List 2-4 things you've been told or believe about health/fitness that you're not sure are true]

Things I'm currently doing or planning to do that I want evaluated:
[Any supplements, specific diets, training approaches, or protocols you're considering]

Help me:
1. What does the evidence consistently show about achieving this specific goal?
2. For each claim I listed: what does the evidence actually say? Is this well-established, partially supported, or mostly marketing?
3. What are the 2-3 things that the evidence shows matter most for my goal — the actual fundamentals?
4. What should I not waste money or energy on right now?
5. Where is genuine uncertainty in the evidence — what's still contested or unclear?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Evidence-based clarity on what matters for your specific goal
- Reality-checked assessment of your beliefs and planned approaches
- Clear list of what to focus on vs. what to deprioritize
- Honest uncertainty map — what science does and doesn't know

## Stage 3: System Design

**Persona: Health Optimizer**
**Time: 40-50 minutes**

Design the actual system — specific, structured, and realistic. Not the optimal system, the one you'll actually follow.

```
I have a clear baseline and an evidence-grounded understanding of what matters. Now help me design the actual system.

What I know from previous stages:
[Summarize: your baseline, the highest-leverage change, the evidence-backed priorities]

Design my system across three components:

**Movement:**
- What training approach fits my goal, schedule, equipment, and starting point?
- How many days per week, what duration, what time of day?
- Specific starting program — not "exercise more," the actual schedule with what to do
- How does this progress over 8-12 weeks?

**Nutrition:**
- What does a realistic eating structure look like for my goal?
- What's my protein target and how do I realistically hit it?
- What's the simplest meal pattern that gets me to the caloric and macro targets?
- Sample day of eating — specific, not aspirational

**Sleep:**
- What's the specific change that would most improve my sleep quality and duration?
- What does the science say about my specific sleep issue (if I have one)?
- What habits do I build around sleep timing, environment, and wind-down?

For each component: give me specific, actionable design — not principles, the actual thing to do.
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- A specific training program with schedule, exercises, and progression
- A nutrition structure with protein target, caloric framework, and sample day
- Specific sleep improvements
- The minimum viable version I can actually start immediately

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Write out my full system: the training schedule, nutrition structure, and sleep changes. Put it in a format I can save and reference."

## Stage 4: Habit Architecture and Commitment

**Persona: Academic Coach, Decision Anchor**
**Time: 25-35 minutes**

The system only works if the behavior holds. This stage designs the habit stack that makes the system survive real life — and gets a clear commitment on what you're actually starting.

```
I have my system. Now I need to make sure it holds — not just in week one, but when it gets hard.

Academic Coach: help me design the habit architecture.

My system summary:
[Training schedule, nutrition approach, sleep changes]

My schedule and life patterns:
[When do you wake up? What do you do first thing? What are your consistent daily anchors?]

What has derailed my previous health attempts:
[Be specific — travel? Late work nights? Exhaustion on certain days? Social meals?]

Help me:
1. Anchor the new behaviors to existing routines — what do I attach each health behavior to so it happens automatically?
2. Design the fail-safe — what's the minimum viable version I do when life goes sideways? (5-minute version of the workout, the default meal, the modified sleep routine)
3. Identify the most likely failure points — when will this break down, and what's the plan for those moments?
4. Set the 30-day benchmark — what does success look like at 30 days? What specific outcomes tell me the system is working?

Decision Anchor: what am I actually committing to?
5. Translate this into three specific commitments — not aspirations, the behaviors I'm promising to myself
6. What starts tomorrow? One thing. Specific.
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- A habit stack with existing routine anchors
- Minimum viable versions for high-stress days
- Identified failure points with pre-planned responses
- Three specific behavioral commitments
- One thing starting tomorrow

## Completion Criteria

Your health system is ready to implement when:

- [ ] Current baseline is honestly assessed — not idealized
- [ ] Evidence has informed the priorities — you know what matters and why
- [ ] Training program is specific — schedule, exercises, progression for 4-8 weeks
- [ ] Nutrition structure is defined — protein target, caloric approach, sample day
- [ ] Sleep improvement is identified and actionable
- [ ] Habit anchors are designed — new behaviors attached to existing routines
- [ ] Failure points are anticipated — minimum viable versions exist for hard days
- [ ] Three specific commitments are made — not intentions, promises to yourself
- [ ] Day-one action is named — one thing, tomorrow

---

## Notes

- The highest-leverage change from Stage 1 is almost always where to start — not everything at once.
- The Stage 3 system should be the minimum viable version, not the optimal one. Build consistency before complexity.
- If the Stage 4 habit audit surfaces a pattern of previous failures, that's not a motivation problem. It's a system design problem. Re-examine the design before recommitting to it.
- See a physician before making significant changes if you have health conditions, medications, or symptoms that need evaluation.
