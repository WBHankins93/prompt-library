# Competitive Landscape Analysis

## What This Workflow Does

Takes you from "I need to understand my competitive environment" to a structured, actionable competitive intelligence picture — with market positioning mapped, strategic gaps identified, and your differentiation grounded in what the market actually shows, not just what you want to believe.

This workflow is for businesses and founders who need a real understanding of the competitive landscape — not a comfort-seeking exercise that confirms existing beliefs, but a rigorous assessment of where you stand and what the market is telling you.

## When to Use This

- You're entering a market and want to understand what you're competing against
- Your competitive situation has changed (new entrant, market shift, category evolution) and you need to re-map the landscape
- You're defining your positioning and need to know what's actually available in the market
- You're raising capital and need a clear, honest competitive narrative
- You're making a product, pricing, or go-to-market decision that depends on understanding the competitive context

## What You'll Need Before Starting

- **What you're competing in** — your product, service, or business and the specific market you're in
- **Who you already know about** — the competitors you're aware of, however incomplete
- **Your current positioning** — how you're describing yourself and your differentiation today
- **What decision this analysis will inform** — the clearer you are about this, the more useful the output

> **New here?** See the [Getting Started guide](getting-started.md) for how to load personas and run each stage.

## How This Workflow Runs

**Run as a single conversation.** Each stage builds the picture the next stage needs. Stage 4's strategic implications are only as useful as Stage 2's honest competitor assessment.

**Estimated time:** 2-3 hours. Most teams find Stage 2 the most time-intensive — and the most valuable.

## Stage 1: Landscape Definition and Framing

**Persona: Pattern Seer**
**Time: 25-35 minutes**

Before you analyze competitors, define the competitive landscape correctly. The way you frame the market determines what you see and what you miss.

```
You are my Pattern Seer — a persona that maps structural forces, competitive dynamics, and the landscape patterns that most people inside a market miss.

I need to analyze the competitive landscape for my business.

What I'm competing in:
[Describe your business, product, or service — what it does, who it's for, what problem it solves]

How I currently think about my competition:
[Who you consider your direct competitors — and how you think the market is structured]

My primary use for this analysis:
[Positioning decision, fundraising narrative, product strategy, go-to-market, or other]

Help me:
1. Define the competitive frame correctly — am I thinking about this market too narrowly or too broadly? Who's actually competing for my customer's decision?
2. Map the competitive categories — direct competitors, indirect alternatives, and the "do nothing" or "build internally" alternatives that buyers often choose
3. Identify the structural forces shaping this market — what trends, technological shifts, or regulatory dynamics are affecting who wins?
4. Name the category this market is in — is it emerging, growing, maturing, or consolidating? What does that stage mean for competitive strategy?
5. Identify what I'm probably missing — given how I've framed it, what competitors or alternatives am I likely not considering?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Correctly framed competitive landscape including indirect alternatives
- Map of competitive categories
- Structural forces shaping the market
- Category stage assessment and its strategic implications

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the competitive landscape framing, categories, structural forces, and category stage. I'll use this to assess specific competitors."

## Stage 2: Competitor Assessment

**Persona: The Researcher, Pattern Seer**
**Time: 55-65 minutes**

The most important stage. An honest, structured assessment of each significant competitor — their positioning, strengths, weaknesses, and the specific customer segments they own.

```
You are my Researcher and Pattern Seer — help me build a rigorous, honest assessment of my competitive landscape.

The landscape frame (from Stage 1):
[Summary of the competitive categories and key competitors to assess]

For each significant competitor (work through them systematically):

**[Competitor name]**
- What they actually do: their positioning, what problems they solve, who they primarily serve
- What they're strong at: where they genuinely win and why
- Where they're weak or failing: where customers complain, where they're not competitive, what they've left on the table
- What customer segment they own: the specific buyer who is best served by them vs. who they're a bad fit for
- Their trajectory: are they getting stronger, weaker, or changing direction?

After working through each competitor:
1. Draw the positioning map — where does each competitor sit on the key axes that matter in this market?
2. Identify the white space — where are customers underserved, and does anyone credibly own that space?
3. Find the patterns — what do the winners have in common? What do the losers have in common?
4. Name the customer segments — how is the customer base actually segmented, and who's winning each segment?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Honest assessment of each significant competitor
- Positioning map across key axes
- White space identification
- Pattern analysis — what's working and what isn't across the landscape

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the competitor assessments, the positioning map, the white space, and the patterns across the landscape."

## Stage 3: Your Positioning and Differentiation

**Persona: Arbitrage Hunter, SaaS Founder**
**Time: 30-40 minutes**

Given the landscape, where do you actually stand? And where is the genuine differentiation opportunity — the position you could own that the market hasn't claimed?

```
You are my Arbitrage Hunter and SaaS Founder — help me understand where I stand in this competitive landscape and where genuine differentiation exists.

My business/product:
[What you do, who you serve, current positioning]

The competitive landscape (from Stages 1 and 2):
[Summary: competitors, positioning map, white space]

Help me:
1. Locate me honestly on the positioning map — where do I currently sit relative to competitors? How is the market currently perceiving me vs. how I want to be perceived?
2. Evaluate my claimed differentiation — for each thing I claim sets me apart, is that genuinely differentiated in this market or does a competitor also claim it?
3. Identify my real differentiation — what is genuinely distinctive about my approach, solution, or team that the market actually values and no one else can easily replicate?
4. Find the position worth owning — based on the white space from Stage 2, what specific position could I credibly own that would be valuable and defensible?
5. Test the differentiation — what would a skeptical buyer say when I claim this differentiation? Is it specific enough to believe?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Honest current positioning assessment
- Reality-check of claimed differentiation
- Identified genuine, defensible differentiation
- A specific position worth pursuing with strategic rationale

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize where I actually stand, what's genuinely differentiated, and the specific position worth owning."

## Stage 4: Strategic Implications and Red Team

**Persona: Red Team, Pattern Seer**
**Time: 25-35 minutes**

Stress-test the analysis and build the specific strategic actions that follow from it.

```
Two parts to this stage.

Red Team first: challenge my competitive analysis and conclusions.

My current competitive conclusions:
[Summarize: market framing, key competitor assessments, my positioning and differentiation claims]

Challenge me:
1. Where is my analysis flattering to myself — where am I underestimating a competitor or overestimating my differentiation?
2. What does my most capable competitor understand about this market that I don't?
3. What scenario makes the white space I identified not actually white space — occupied, unprofitable, or structurally inaccessible?
4. What's the competitive move I'm not taking seriously that a smart competitor is planning?

Then Pattern Seer: strategic implications.
5. Given this honest competitive picture, what are the 2-3 highest-leverage strategic actions?
6. What should I stop doing because it's not differentiated?
7. What's the competitive narrative — the honest version of what I'd tell a customer or investor about where I fit and why I win?
8. What competitive signals should I be monitoring over the next 6-12 months?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Red Team-tested competitive analysis
- Identified blind spots and competitor threats
- 2-3 highest-leverage strategic actions
- A competitive narrative you can actually use
- Monitoring signals for the next 6-12 months

---

## Completion Criteria

The competitive analysis is complete when:

- [ ] Competitive landscape is correctly framed — including indirect alternatives
- [ ] Each significant competitor is honestly assessed — not just listed
- [ ] Positioning map exists — visual or described, showing where each player sits
- [ ] White space is identified — where customers are underserved
- [ ] Your differentiation is honest — passes the skeptical buyer test
- [ ] Red Team has challenged the conclusions
- [ ] Strategic actions are specific — not "improve positioning" but specific moves
- [ ] Competitive narrative is ready — one you'd actually say to a customer or investor

---

## Notes

- The most common mistake is too narrow a competitive frame. Define competitors as buyers think about their alternatives, not as you think about your peers.
- "We have no competitors" is almost never true. What it usually means is "we haven't mapped the landscape correctly yet."
- The competitor assessment stage (Stage 2) is the most time-consuming and most valuable. Don't rush it.
- Revisit this analysis whenever the market shifts significantly — new entrants, funding rounds, product announcements, or customer behavior changes.
