# Skill Learning Fast Track

## What This Workflow Does

Takes you from "I want to learn X" to a structured, evidence-based learning system — with a realistic timeline, a practice architecture designed for how humans actually retain skills, and a clear starting point that isn't "watch more YouTube videos."

Most skill acquisition fails not from lack of effort but from lack of structure: too much passive consumption, too little deliberate practice, no feedback loop, and no clear sense of what progress actually looks like at each stage.

## When to Use This

- You're learning a skill for a specific purpose (career change, promotion, side project, personal goal) and want to move fast
- You've tried to learn something before and it didn't stick — you want to understand why before trying again
- You're not sure whether this skill is worth your time and want clarity before investing months in it
- You have limited time available and need to prioritize — not everything, the highest-leverage moves
- You want to design a practice system that will hold when motivation drops

## What You'll Need Before Starting

- **The skill you want to learn** — as specifically as possible; "coding" is too broad, "building web apps with Python and Django" is workable
- **Why this skill, why now** — the purpose behind the goal shapes what "good enough" looks like and how to sequence learning
- **Your current baseline** — what you already know that's adjacent or relevant; prior knowledge dramatically changes the learning path
- **Your real time constraints** — how many hours per week, for how long, under what conditions

> **New here?** See the [Getting Started guide](getting-started.md) for how to load personas and run each stage.

## How This Workflow Runs

**Run as a single conversation.** The learning architecture from Stage 2 is only as good as the goal clarity from Stage 1. The practice system from Stage 3 only works if it's designed for your specific constraints.

**Estimated time:** 2-3 hours. Stage 3 is the most design-intensive — the practice architecture is where most skill-learning attempts either succeed or fail.

## Stage 1: Skill Definition and Goal Clarity

**Persona: Academic Coach, Wayfinder**
**Time: 30-40 minutes**

Before designing a learning path, define what you're actually trying to learn and why. Vague goals produce vague systems that produce no progress.

```
You are my Academic Coach and Wayfinder — help me get precise about what I'm trying to learn and why it matters.

The skill I want to learn:
[Name the skill as specifically as you can — the more specific, the better the learning path]

Why this skill, why now:
[What's the purpose? Career goal, project requirement, personal interest, solving a specific problem?]

What I already know that's adjacent:
[Any prior knowledge, related skills, or experience that might transfer]

My real constraints:
- Time available: [Hours per week you can genuinely dedicate]
- Timeline: [When do you need to reach a useful level?]
- Learning conditions: [When and where do you actually practice — morning, commute, evenings, dedicated blocks?]

Help me:
1. Sharpen the skill definition — am I thinking about this too broadly or too narrowly? What's the right scope given my goal?
2. Define what "good enough" looks like — at 30 days, 90 days, and 6 months, what specific capability would tell me the learning is on track?
3. Reality-check the timeline — given my constraints, is my timeline realistic? What's the realistic ceiling given the time I have?
4. Name the specific use case — what will I actually do with this skill? The answer shapes which parts of the skill matter most.
5. Identify what I already know that transfers — where is my prior knowledge an asset in this learning journey?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- A precisely defined skill with appropriate scope
- Milestone definitions at 30 days, 90 days, and 6 months
- A reality-checked timeline grounded in actual time available
- The specific use case that determines which parts of the skill to prioritize

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the skill definition, milestone targets, and timeline reality. I'll use this to build the learning architecture."

## Stage 2: Learning Architecture

**Persona: Academic Coach, Curious Scientist**
**Time: 40-50 minutes**

Most people learn inefficiently — too much passive consumption, not enough active practice, no spaced repetition, no deliberate focus on the hardest parts. This stage designs the architecture that actually builds skill.

```
You are my Academic Coach and Curious Scientist — help me design a learning architecture based on how humans actually acquire skills.

The skill and goal (from Stage 1):
[Skill definition, milestone targets, specific use case]

Help me:
1. Map the skill's component structure — what are the distinct sub-skills that make up this skill? Which are foundational (must learn first) vs. advanced (build on foundations)?
2. Identify the critical path — given my specific use case, what's the minimum set of sub-skills that gets me to useful capability fastest? What can I defer?
3. Find the best learning resources — for this specific skill, what are the highest-quality resources (courses, books, tutorials, communities)? What makes them higher quality than the alternatives?
4. Design the learning sequence — in what order should I work through the components? What's the specific starting point for day one?
5. Apply the evidence on skill acquisition:
   - What's the role of deliberate practice in this skill? What does deliberate practice actually look like here, not just "practice more"?
   - How does spaced repetition apply to this skill's knowledge components?
   - What's the research on passive learning (watching, reading) vs. active practice — what ratio is right for this kind of skill?
6. Name the plateau points — where do most learners get stuck in this skill, and why? What's the path through those specific obstacles?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Component map of the skill with foundational vs. advanced structure
- Critical path to useful capability
- Curated resource list with rationale
- Evidence-based learning sequence with day-one starting point
- Known plateau points and how to navigate them

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the skill structure, critical path, resource recommendations, and learning sequence. I'll use this to design my practice system."

## Stage 3: Practice System Design

**Persona: Academic Coach**
**Time: 40-50 minutes**

A learning architecture is theory. A practice system is what you actually do every day. This stage designs the specific sessions, feedback loops, and progression structure that builds real skill.

```
I have my learning architecture. Now help me design the practice system — the specific sessions and structures that will build the skill.

What I know from previous stages:
[Skill definition, component structure, critical path, resource recommendations, learning sequence]

My practice constraints:
- Session length: [How long can a typical practice session actually be?]
- Weekly frequency: [How many days per week?]
- Best available time: [Morning, evening, lunch — when am I actually sharp enough for hard practice?]
- Environment: [Where will I practice — at a desk, on a commute, in short bursts?]

Design my practice system:

**Session Structure:**
- What does a typical practice session look like, minute by minute? Not principles — the actual session structure.
- What's the ratio of passive learning (reading, watching) to active practice for this skill at my current stage?
- What's the warm-up routine that gets me into deliberate practice quickly?

**Feedback Loops:**
- How do I know if I'm actually improving? What are the concrete signals that tell me the practice is working?
- How do I get feedback on my practice when I don't have a coach or teacher?
- What's the self-assessment check I run at the end of each session?

**Progression Structure:**
- How does difficulty increase over the first 90 days? What's the trigger to move from one level to the next?
- What does the 30-day practice plan look like specifically — what am I doing in weeks 1, 2, 3, and 4?
- What are the milestones that tell me I've actually reached each capability level?

**The Minimum Viable Practice:**
- What's the smallest practice unit that still produces skill development — the 15-minute version I do when life is full?
- What never gets cut no matter how busy things get?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- A specific session structure with time allocation
- Concrete feedback mechanisms for self-directed learning
- 30-day practice plan with week-by-week progression
- Minimum viable practice for constrained days
- Milestone definitions with clear assessment criteria

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Write out my complete practice system: the session structure, the 30-day plan, the feedback loops, and the minimum viable version."

## Stage 4: Commitment and Obstacle Architecture

**Persona: Decision Anchor, Academic Coach**
**Time: 25-35 minutes**

The system only holds if the commitment is specific and the obstacles are anticipated. This stage converts the practice design into real commitments and builds the failure architecture before the failures happen.

```
I have my practice system. Now I need to make it stick — not just in week one, but when it gets hard.

Decision Anchor: lock in the commitment.

My practice system summary:
[Session structure, weekly schedule, 30-day plan]

1. What am I actually committing to — not aspirationally, but specifically? Three behavioral commitments in concrete language: days, times, locations, durations.
2. What's the minimum I will hold no matter what — the floor behavior that keeps the habit alive on the worst weeks?
3. When will I formally assess my progress and decide whether to continue, adjust, or stop? Set the first review date and the criteria.

Academic Coach: build the obstacle architecture.

What has derailed my previous learning attempts:
[Be specific — time pressure, motivation drops, getting stuck on hard concepts, social pressure, shiny new goals?]

4. Anticipate the three most likely failure points — when will this break down, and what's the specific plan for those moments?
5. Design the restart protocol — not if I miss a week, but when. What's the specific sequence I follow to restart without shame spiral or lost momentum?
6. Name the accountability structure — how will I know if this is working at 30 days? What specific outcome or capability test tells me yes or no?
7. What starts tomorrow? One thing. Specific. Time. Location.
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Three specific behavioral commitments (not intentions)
- The minimum viable practice floor
- Scheduled first progress assessment with criteria
- Anticipated failure points with pre-planned responses
- A restart protocol for when (not if) disruptions happen
- One thing starting tomorrow

## Completion Criteria

The learning system is ready to implement when:

- [ ] Skill is precisely defined — scoped to a specific use case, not a broad category
- [ ] Milestones exist — specific capability descriptions at 30 days, 90 days, 6 months
- [ ] Timeline is reality-checked — grounded in actual available hours, not aspiration
- [ ] Component structure is mapped — foundational vs. advanced sub-skills identified
- [ ] Critical path is defined — the minimum set of sub-skills to reach useful capability
- [ ] Resources are curated — specific, quality-filtered, not a pile of links
- [ ] Session structure is specific — not "practice daily" but time, structure, content
- [ ] Feedback loops exist — concrete ways to know if the practice is working
- [ ] 30-day plan is written — week-by-week progression, not just a general approach
- [ ] Three behavioral commitments are made — specific, not aspirational
- [ ] Failure architecture is built — obstacles anticipated, restart protocol ready
- [ ] Day-one action is named — one thing, tomorrow, specific

## Notes

- Passive learning feels like learning but often isn't. The ratio of active practice to passive consumption should increase as the skill develops — start with enough passive to understand what you're doing, then shift heavily toward practice.
- Most learners quit at the first competence plateau — the point where beginner wins stop coming and intermediate difficulty begins. Knowing this plateau is coming and naming when to expect it significantly improves persistence.
- "I don't have time" is almost always a prioritization problem, not a time problem. The Stage 4 commitment architecture works best when it's honest about what the skill is competing with for attention.
- The restart protocol (Stage 4) is not optional. Disruptions are certain. The learners who build lasting skills are the ones who restart quickly, not the ones who never stop.
- Revisit this workflow when the goal shifts significantly, when a plateau has persisted for more than 4-6 weeks, or when life changes have made the original practice system unworkable.
