# Research Deep Dive Workflow

## What This Workflow Does

Takes you from "I want to understand X thoroughly" to a structured, well-sourced, honestly uncertain understanding of a topic — with the contested claims named, the source quality evaluated, and the gaps in current knowledge acknowledged.

This isn't a summary. It's a research process: building a real understanding of what's established, what's debated, what's unknown, and what you can act on with confidence.

## When to Use This

- You need to deeply understand a topic before making a high-stakes decision
- You want to evaluate competing claims and understand which have real evidence behind them
- You're investigating a market, technology, regulatory environment, or research area
- You've read the popular summaries and want to understand what's actually underneath them
- You want to distinguish between what experts actually believe and what's been simplified for public consumption

## What You'll Need Before Starting

- **The question you're actually trying to answer** — be specific; "understand AI" is not a research question; "understand which AI deployment approaches have the best evidence for ROI in small teams" is
- **Why this matters** — what decision or understanding are you building toward? This shapes what level of depth is appropriate
- **What you already know** — your starting point determines where to begin

> **New here?** See the [Getting Started guide](getting-started.md) for how to load personas and run each stage.

## How This Workflow Runs

**Run as a single conversation.** The Researcher's strength is its ability to build a coherent picture across stages — each stage adds structure to what came before.

For ongoing research projects, use the checkpoint system to save progress and return across multiple sessions.

**Estimated time:** 2-3 hours. Complex topics may require multiple sessions; use checkpoints to maintain continuity.

---

## Stage 1: Scope and Question Definition

**Persona: The Researcher**
**Time: 20-30 minutes**

Vague questions produce vague answers. Before any research, get precise about what you're actually trying to understand.

```
You are my Researcher — a persona that conducts rigorous investigation of contested or complex topics, evaluates sources with precision, and builds understanding at the appropriate level of confidence.

I want to do a deep research dive on a topic. Before we start, help me define the question precisely.

The topic I want to understand:
[What you want to research — as specific as you can be at this point]

Why this matters to me / what I'm building toward:
[The decision, project, or understanding this research is in service of]

What I already know or believe about this topic:
[Your current understanding — including what you're not sure about]

What I want to be able to do or decide when we're done:
[The practical end state — what should this research enable?]

Help me:
1. Sharpen the question — convert the general topic into 2-3 specific, answerable research questions
2. Scope the investigation — what are the necessary sub-questions? What's out of scope for this session?
3. Map my prior beliefs — which of my current assumptions are most likely to be tested by rigorous investigation?
4. Define the uncertainty budget — given the topic, what level of certainty is actually achievable? Am I expecting more precision than is available?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- 2-3 specific research questions
- Scoped investigation with clear in/out of scope
- Named prior beliefs to test
- Calibrated expectations for the achievable level of certainty

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the specific research questions, scope, and my prior beliefs to test. I'll use this to structure the next stages."

## Stage 2: Evidence Landscape

**Persona: The Researcher, Curious Scientist**
**Time: 50-60 minutes**

Build the full landscape of what's known, what's debated, and what's unknown — with each claim assigned to the appropriate confidence tier.

```
You are my Researcher and Curious Scientist — build the evidence landscape for my research questions.

My research questions (from Stage 1):
[List the 2-3 specific questions]

For each research question, help me understand:

1. What's established — what does the evidence consistently show? What claims have strong, replicated support from multiple independent sources?

2. What's supported but contested — what claims have substantial evidence but also credible counter-evidence or unresolved debate among experts?

3. What's claimed but unverified — what's widely stated but lacks rigorous evidential support? What's being repeated as established that isn't?

4. What's genuinely unknown — what aspects of this topic don't have good answers yet? Where do the experts themselves say they don't know?

5. Source quality assessment — for the key claims in each tier, what types of evidence are they based on? (Primary research, meta-analyses, expert consensus, observational data, expert opinion, popular claims)

6. The most important thing I probably have wrong — given my prior beliefs, what's most likely to be an incorrect assumption?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Structured evidence landscape across all four confidence tiers
- Source quality assessment for key claims
- Named incorrect prior beliefs where evidence differs
- A map of genuine unknowns and contested areas

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the evidence landscape: what's established, what's contested, what's claimed but unverified, and what's genuinely unknown for my research questions."

## Stage 3: Synthesis and Pattern Identification

**Persona: Pattern Seer, The Researcher**
**Time: 35-45 minutes**

Evidence without synthesis is a pile of information. This stage builds the higher-order understanding — what do the patterns mean, what's the underlying structure, and what does this imply for decisions?

```
You are my Pattern Seer and Researcher — help me synthesize what I've found into higher-order understanding.

The evidence landscape from Stage 2:
[Ask the AI to recall what was built, or summarize if needed]

Help me:
1. Identify the underlying structure — what's the core mechanism or dynamic at work here? What does the evidence point to as the root system beneath the surface claims?

2. Find the patterns that cut across the research questions — what shows up consistently across different sub-questions or evidence types?

3. Identify the expert consensus (where one exists) vs. the popular narrative — where does what the evidence actually shows diverge from what's in popular circulation?

4. Construct the "most likely true" model — given everything, what's the best current understanding that accounts for the full evidence landscape?

5. Map the implications — given the most likely true model, what does this suggest about [your specific decision/use case from Stage 1]?

6. Identify what would change the model — what new evidence or research would substantially change the current best understanding?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- A synthesized, structured understanding of the topic
- Expert consensus vs. popular narrative clearly distinguished
- A "most likely true" model appropriate to the evidence
- Practical implications for your specific situation
- What would change the model

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the synthesis: the underlying structure, the most likely true model, the key patterns, and the practical implications for my situation."

## Stage 4: Gaps, Limitations, and Actionable Output

**Persona: Red Team, The Researcher**
**Time: 25-35 minutes**

Honest research ends with honest uncertainty — and with the specific output you can act on.

```
Two parts to this stage.

Red Team first: challenge my research conclusions.

My current understanding (from Stages 2 and 3):
[Brief summary of what I now believe to be true]

Challenge me:
1. Where am I overconfident — where does my conclusion outrun the actual evidence?
2. What did I look for that confirmed what I already believed — where might confirmation bias have shaped my conclusions?
3. What's the strongest credible counterargument to my main conclusions?
4. What would an expert critic of this research synthesis say I'm missing?

Then Researcher: build the actionable output.
5. A confidence-tiered summary — what I can act on with high confidence, what I should hold tentatively, and what remains genuinely uncertain
6. The 3-5 most important things to understand about this topic for someone in my situation
7. Recommended next steps — if I want to go deeper, where? What sources, questions, or investigations would most improve my understanding?
8. A one-paragraph synthesis I could share with someone else — what's the honest, accurate, appropriately uncertain version of what I now know?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Confidence-tiered, Red Team-tested summary
- Honest uncertainty acknowledged — what you know vs. believe vs. don't know
- Actionable output appropriate to your situation
- A shareable synthesis paragraph
- Recommended next steps for continued investigation

---

## Completion Criteria

The research is done when:

- [ ] Research questions are specific and answerable
- [ ] Evidence landscape is built — established, contested, claimed, unknown
- [ ] Synthesis has a "most likely true" model with appropriate confidence
- [ ] Red Team has challenged the conclusions
- [ ] Confidence is calibrated — not higher than the evidence warrants
- [ ] Actionable output is clear — what you can act on and what you hold tentatively
- [ ] Honest unknowns are named — not papered over

---

## Notes

- The most valuable output of this workflow is usually the evidence landscape from Stage 2 — specifically what's claimed but unverified. That's where most popular understanding is wrong.
- The workflow is only as good as the questions. A vague question in Stage 1 produces a vague research output. Time spent sharpening the question is the highest-ROI step.
- For topics where the primary literature is the key source, the Researcher will help you identify what to find — you may need to go read the actual sources and return with what you find for the synthesis stages.
