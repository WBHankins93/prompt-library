# Home Purchase Due Diligence Workflow

## What This Workflow Does

Takes you from "I want to make an offer" or "I'm under contract" to a clear-eyed decision: buy at this price with these conditions, negotiate harder, or walk away.

Most homebuyers evaluate properties through the lens of what they want to be true. This workflow forces the questions that reveal what's actually true — about the physical condition, the financial reality, and the risks that don't show up in the listing photos.

## When to Use This

- You're about to make an offer and want to know what to look for before committing
- You're under contract and in your inspection/due diligence period
- You received an inspection report and don't know how to interpret what's significant
- You're deciding whether to negotiate on findings, ask for repairs, or walk
- You're a first-time buyer who doesn't know what you don't know

## What You'll Need Before Starting

- **The property details** — address, listing price, age, size, property type (single-family, condo, etc.)
- **What you know about the home's condition** — listing description, your own walk-through observations, any disclosure documents
- **The financial picture** — your offer price or purchase price, your financing, your cash reserves
- **The inspection report, if you have one** — paste key findings or the full report if available

> **New here?** See the [Getting Started guide](getting-started.md) for how to load personas and run each stage.

## How This Workflow Runs

**Run in two modes:** Before an offer (Stages 1 and 2 are most useful as a pre-offer checklist), or during the due diligence period (all four stages with the actual inspection report in hand).

**Estimated time:** 2-3 hours. Stage 1 can be quick if you're pre-offer; Stage 2 requires the actual inspection report.

## Stage 1: Property Assessment and Red Flags

**Persona: Property Analyst**
**Time: 30-45 minutes**

The Property Analyst sees properties the way a seasoned inspector does — evaluating structure, systems, and the patterns that indicate hidden problems. This stage establishes what you know, what you should look for, and what would make this property a pass.

```
You are my Property Analyst — a licensed home inspector persona that reads properties for structural integrity, system condition, and the patterns that indicate hidden or developing problems.

I'm evaluating a property I'm considering purchasing.

Property details:
[Address, property type, age of home, square footage, any information about major systems (roof age, HVAC age, etc.)]

What I've observed or know about the condition:
[Your walk-through impressions, anything the seller disclosed, any obvious concerns you noticed]

The listing description says:
[Paste relevant condition info from the listing, or note "nothing specific"]

Inspection report (if available):
[Paste or summarize key findings]

Help me:
1. Interpret what I know — given the age and type of this home, what are the most likely problem areas I should be paying attention to?
2. The critical items — if I have an inspection report, what rises to the level of structural or safety concern vs. deferred maintenance vs. cosmetic?
3. The hidden risk patterns — what does this home's age, type, and what I've observed suggest about systems that may be near end of life?
4. What to look for in the inspection — if I haven't had an inspection yet, what specifically should I ask the inspector to look carefully at?
5. The deal-breaker check — what findings would make this property a hard pass at any reasonable price? What findings would be negotiable?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Clear categorization of findings: critical vs. deferred maintenance vs. cosmetic
- Identified hidden risk patterns for this property type and age
- A deal-breaker checklist specific to what you know
- Specific questions for your inspector if you haven't had one yet

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the property condition assessment: critical findings, deferred maintenance, hidden risks, and what would make this a pass. I'll use this in the negotiation stage."

## Stage 2: Financial Reality

**Persona: Finance Dragon**
**Time: 30-40 minutes**

The purchase price isn't the cost of this home. The cost is the purchase price plus the mortgage over its life, plus estimated repairs and maintenance, plus carrying costs. This stage builds the full financial picture.

```
You are my Finance Dragon — you model the full financial reality of major purchases, including costs that aren't obvious from the listing price.

I'm evaluating the purchase of a home.

Purchase price (or offer price I'm considering):
[Dollar amount]

My financing:
[Down payment amount, approximate interest rate and loan type, if known]

What I know about the condition and likely repairs:
[Summary from Stage 1, or your best estimate of near-term repair needs]

My financial situation:
[Approximate monthly income, existing debt payments, cash reserves after the down payment]

Help me:
1. Model the true monthly cost — PITI (principal, interest, taxes, insurance) plus realistic monthly maintenance reserve (not the listing estimate)
2. Identify the hidden costs — what does a buyer of this property type and age typically not budget for?
3. The repair and deferred maintenance cost estimate — based on what we know, what should I budget for year-one and years 2-5?
4. Affordability check — does this purchase leave me financially resilient or fragile? What does my cash position look like after close?
5. The negotiation math — if there are significant findings, what's the dollar value of repair credits I should request? What's my justification?
6. The alternative math — rent vs. buy, or this home vs. renting and waiting
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- True total monthly cost including maintenance reserves
- Budget for near-term and medium-term repairs
- Post-close financial picture and cash position
- Specific dollar amount for any repair credit negotiation

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the financial picture: true monthly cost, repair budgets, cash position post-close, and my negotiation target if applicable."

## Stage 3: Legal and Contract Review

**Persona: Legal Advisor**
**Time: 20-30 minutes**

What the purchase agreement says matters. This stage focuses on the key provisions that protect you — and the common places buyers get exposed.

```
You are my Legal Advisor — you help me understand the legal landscape of a real estate transaction and what protections I should ensure are in place.

I'm under contract (or about to go under contract) on a home purchase.

Contract details I know:
[Contingencies in place — inspection, financing, appraisal? Closing date? Any unusual provisions?]

State I'm purchasing in:
[State matters for legal context — disclosure laws vary significantly]

Specific concerns from the inspection or disclosure:
[Anything that came up that might have legal implications — easements, permits, neighbor disputes, unpermitted work]

Help me understand:
1. Contingency protection — are my contingencies structured to actually protect me? What would I need to do to invoke them?
2. Disclosure risks — what should the seller have disclosed in this state? Is there anything that should have been disclosed that wasn't?
3. Unpermitted work implications — if there's any, what are the buyer's risks and liabilities?
4. Title concerns — what should I ask my title company to look for given what I know about this property?
5. What happens if I walk — under my current contract, what are the conditions under which I can exit and recover my earnest money?

Note: This is for general understanding. My real estate attorney and agent are my advisors for anything high-stakes.
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- Understanding of your contingency protections
- Identified disclosure risks for this state and situation
- Clarity on any unpermitted work implications
- Questions for your title company and attorney

**Optional checkpoint:**
> "Summarize the key legal protections I have and the legal concerns I need to follow up on with my agent and attorney."

## Stage 4: Decision and Negotiation Strategy

**Persona: Property Analyst, Red Team**
**Time: 25-35 minutes**

All the information is in. This stage synthesizes it into a clear decision and, if proceeding, a specific negotiation strategy.

```
I've completed my due diligence. Now I need to decide: proceed at current terms, negotiate, or walk.

Everything I know:
[Summarize the property condition, financial picture, and legal situation from previous stages]

My current lean:
[Proceed / negotiate / walk — and why]

Red Team: challenge my reasoning:
1. Am I rationalizing a property I want rather than evaluating one clearly?
2. What am I discounting or ignoring that I shouldn't?
3. If I'm leaning toward proceeding — what's the scenario where this turns into an expensive mistake?
4. If I'm leaning toward passing — am I being too cautious, or is the caution warranted?

Then Property Analyst: if I'm proceeding or negotiating:
5. What's my negotiation position? What specifically should I ask for — price reduction, repair credit, repairs completed before close?
6. What's the minimum I should accept to stay in the deal?
7. What's the walkaway line?
```

**What you'll have at the end of this stage:**
- A clear decision: proceed as-is, negotiate, or walk
- If negotiating: specific ask, minimum acceptable, and walkaway terms
- Red Team has challenged your reasoning

---

## Completion Criteria

Your due diligence is complete when:

- [ ] Property condition is understood — critical items, deferred maintenance, hidden risks
- [ ] True financial cost is modeled — including repairs, reserves, and post-close cash position
- [ ] Legal protections are in place — contingencies, disclosure review, title
- [ ] Red Team has challenged the buy thesis
- [ ] Decision is clear — proceed at these terms, negotiate this specific thing, or walk with these reasons
- [ ] If negotiating: specific dollar ask is ready with justification

---

## Notes

- This workflow works best when you have a real inspection report to work from. Pre-offer use is valuable but less precise.
- Never waive an inspection contingency without understanding exactly what you're giving up.
- The financial stage is where most buyers discover their "affordable" home isn't. Do this before your emotions are fully attached to the property.
- This workflow doesn't replace your actual real estate attorney, inspector, or agent. It prepares you to ask them better questions.
